Big data: Passing Fad or Here to Stay?

Why Big Data isn’t Going Anywhere and How to Use it to Your Advantage

by Victoria Walsh — Posted in Technology on October 17th, 2014

Everyone seems to be talking about big data – it’s like that hit pop song that plays on your car radio, office radio, and then your television at home. It’s hard to get away from and a lot of people are saying it’s a fad.

On the contrary though, big data is an important component of business, big and small, and is expected to take on an even larger role in future. Gartner predicts big data will reach mainstream adoption in 5 to 10 years.

Real-World Applications

If you're still unsure what this "buzzword" means, here's the gist of it: big data describes high-volume structured and unstructured data that is so large and moves so fast it requires advanced processing. Big data provides enhanced insights, helping companies operate more efficiently and make faster, smarter decisions.

“There is a big data revolution,” says Weatherhead University Professor Gary King. But it is not the quantity of data that is revolutionary. “The big data revolution is that now we can  do  something with the data.”

Though it’s expected to take at least another 5 years before enterprises fully embrace big data initiatives, big data is already making an impact today and streaming from everywhere – systems, social media, mobile devices, and sensors.

Online retailers use big data to make recommendations while you shop, helpfully pointing out that "Others who purchased this item also bought...," or offering special discounts based on data from your past purchases.

Big data is used to analyze supply chain logistics. By combining GPS and traffic information, decisions can be made on the fly about the best route for a delivery driver to take, saving on fuel and ensuring goods make it the client on time.

Have an energy leak in your facility? Big data will let you know. MIT is currently utilizing sensors and analytic software to make buildings across its campus more efficient, saving money and reducing its carbon footprint.

If you've seen Moneyball you may not have realized that big data was at play there too. The film is based on the true story of how the Oakland Athletics' management used in-depth analytical evidence of player performance to assemble a competitive team, despite a limited budget.
